Slide 02 ELI5

The engine in three jobs.

Before the numbers, the mental model. The engine is three people working together on every bar.

Librarian (1A)

Given today's chart, goes and finds the most similar charts from history. Like Shazam for price patterns. 1A asks: is the librarian using too many expensive tricks to find matches, or would a simpler one work just as well?

Weatherman (1B)

Given 50 historical charts that look like today, draws a fan of what tended to happen next. 1B asks: can the weatherman learn from his recent misses — "last week my 80% range only caught 55%, so I should widen it" — instead of always using the same fixed confidence?

Advisor (1C)

Given the fan, decides whether to actually act. Checks: is the weatherman calibrated lately? Do the historical charts actually agree? Have we seen weather like this before? 1C asks: does that advisor exist as code, end to end?

The loop: librarian finds analogues → weatherman draws the fan → advisor decides whether and how much to act → review the decision later. Every finding below is about one of these three jobs.

Slide 04 Finding 1 — Retrieval bench Discard (prelim)

Tier 2 is a 37× runtime sink and we can't see it helping on SPY.

Walk-forward bench, two arms (Tier 1 only vs Tier 1 + full 9-method stack), three SPY slices (bull 2016-2019, COVID 2020, rate-hike 2022), seed=42, 8 trials per slice.

Slice ΔCRPS (t1+2 − t1) Corr Δ Runtime × Tier 1
spy-bull-2016-2019 ≈ 0 −0.06 10.6×
spy-covid-2020 ≈ 0 −0.18 46.0×
spy-rate-hike-2022 ≈ 0 −0.12 54.3×
Mean +0.00004 −0.12 37×
All four lane gates fail: runtime > 3× budget, CRPS doesn't improve on any slice, correlation drops, calibration flat.

Engine defaults unchanged. This is measurement, not replacement. The budget-expanded rerun (NVDA, TSLA, BTC, seed=314, regime-transition slices) is queued before we touch defaults.

Slide 05 Finding 1 — what it means

The correlation dropped. Tier 2 is not just expensive — on this slice set, it's adversarial.

This is the detail worth chewing on. Tier 2 didn't just fail to help; it pulled rank order away from the forward-return-useful matches that Tier 1 correctly identified.

Hypothesis 1

Tier 2 weights are mis-tuned. The scorer assigns fixed weights per method across regimes. On SPY those weights actively down-rank windows Tier 1 got right.

Hypothesis 2

Per-regime weight drift. Wavelet scale exponents and EMD IMFs shift meaning across regimes; one weight per method across all regimes cannot capture this.

Hypothesis 3

Tier 2 is under-tuned for daily SPY bars. Wavelet leaders in particular want longer windows and calmer signals than we hand them.

Do not act on this yet. Strong claim, thin evidence (3 slices, 1 seed, SPY only). Acting before the expanded rerun would break the same discipline the bench just created.
Slide 06 Finding 2 — Projector v2 Keep (pending real data)

Adaptive conformal is the biggest single-variable projector win we've measured.

Walk-forward sweep, SPY-1d + BTC-1d, 15 trials per cell, window=50, forward_bars=20. Four variants against the weighted-quantile baseline. Mean numbers across both slices:

Variant ΔCRPS ΔCal err ΔJoint-CRPS Verdict
adaptive_conformal −14% −0.033 ≈ 0 Keep
change_aware_conformal −14% −0.033 ≈ 0 Keep*
joint_path −2.8% −0.017 slight + Marginal
regime_aware_widening +2.8% ≈ 0 slight + Discard

* change-aware is numerically identical to adaptive on smooth data. Needs a shift-rich slice (COVID entry, VIX spike, rate-hike week) to prove the CUSUM reset does something the plain variant doesn't.

Slide 07 Finding 2 — why it matters

Calibration is the projector's job. Adaptive conformal adds the feedback loop we didn't have.

The baseline projector has no online mechanism from recent miscoverage to next-cone-width. Adaptive conformal (Gibbs & Candès, 2021) updates the effective α online based on recent coverage error — widens when under-covered, narrows when over-covered. It wraps any underlying projector, so future projector upgrades still benefit from it.

This is the most portable upgrade in the repo. If we later replace the weighted-quantile cone with a learned one, adaptive conformal still applies on top. The win compounds.

Why not promote to default yet

The v1 sweep ran on synthetic-fallback data, not real parquets. Real market returns have fatter tails, vol clustering, and regime shifts. A variant that wins on synthetic can fail on real.

What unlocks the flip

Real-parquet confirmation sweep on SPY / BTC / NVDA + one crisis slice, followed by a window-length sweep. Then promote behind Config.projector.

Slide 08 Finding 3 — Decision layer Shipped (opt-in)

Search → project → trust-gate → decide → review.

Track 1C added three modules, all opt-in, none change engine defaults.

TrustFilter

Four signals: calibration MAE (hard gate), match-pool agreement (soft), regime novelty (soft), sample size (hard). Emits TrustDecision.

CalibrationAwareStrategy

Wraps any Strategy. Veto if untrusted. Tail-percentile entry gate. Size ∝ trust × confidence.

ReviewSummary

Plain-text audit per decision: signals → gate states → sizing reasoning → outcome. The "review" step of the loop.

Reference workflow in examples/finance_workflow_v1.py: load → backtest → search → project → trust-gate → decide → review, end-to-end on sample data.

Slide 09 Finding 3 — pilot spec

Written shape of a finance pilot. Not yet a pilot.

Target user: small fund / prop trader, $5M–$500M AUM, daily equities, 2–10 day hold. Success is measurable, not just aspirational:

Gate Target Why this bar
Coverage vs stated within 5pp A user who can't trust the cone can't trust the product.
Directional hit rate ≥ 55% Above-random on gated calls.
Veto rate 20–40% Never vetoing = cosmetic gate. Always vetoing = no product.
Sharpe lift vs naive ≥ 0.3 Improvement over a naive threshold on the same cone.
Being honest about what 1C is. It is a runnable technical prototype of the decision loop. It is not a polished UI, an identified design partner, a tested price point, or a signed pilot. The spec defines what a pilot would look like; we haven't put it in front of anyone. Calling it pitch-ready would be wrong. Calling it pilot-shaped is right.
Slide 10 Synthesis

The three findings fit one sentence of strategy.

The plan's thesis is finance first, engine first, calibration over aesthetics. Phase 1 says:

  1. 1A: We may be spending too much on Tier 2 retrieval. Consider demoting from default — after the expanded rerun confirms.
  2. 1B: Adaptive conformal is the projector win to chase. Confirm on real data, then promote behind a flag.
  3. 1C: The trust filter + decision rules turn "a cone" into "a decision with provenance." That's the pilot surface.

Phase 1 is now genuinely measurable. The engine has a scorecard it didn't have yesterday.

Slide 11 What this doesn't prove

What we still don't know.

  • Tier 2 on non-equity assets (BTC, ETH, FX) and cross-asset retrieval.
  • Tier 2 on regime transitions specifically, not just regime interiors.
  • Adaptive conformal on real market data, not synthetic fallback.
  • Adaptive conformal across regime breaks (where change-aware should diverge).
  • The right coverage window length for adaptive conformal (fixed at 50 trials now).
  • Trust-filter weights and thresholds against real P&L, not toy data.
  • Whether the four trust signals are the right four — crowding and information-freshness are candidates to add.

These are the questions the expanded bench, the foundation-model lane, and the first pilot run are designed to answer.
